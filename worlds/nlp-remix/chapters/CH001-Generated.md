The rain against the glass of the Agency office didn't just streak; it screamed.

Detective Arrow stood by the window, watching the neon-slicked gorge of the Terminal District below. The storm was heavy tonight, a static hiss that usually drowned out the city’s thoughts. But for a second, the water running down the pane didn’t look like water. It pooled, twisted, and formed a mouth. A wide, silent, screaming mouth.

Arrow blinked. He focused on the glass, narrowing his eyes—his standard calibration. *Structure. Substance. Reality.*

The face dissolved back into innocent rivulets of grey rain.

"Pareidolia," he muttered to the empty room. "Pattern recognition error. Processor fatigue."

He turned back to his desk. It was a slab of matte-black composite, cluttered only by an ashtray and the glowing blue eye of the Nexus terminal. The office was quiet, save for the hum of the servers in the walls—the nervous system of his operation. He was a linguistic detective, a decoder of deep structures in a city that had forgotten how to speak the truth.

The door chimed. A soft, synthetic note that meant a client with credits.

"Enter," Arrow said.

The woman who walked in was a masterpiece of high-resolution rendering. She wore a coat the color of midnight, cut sharp enough to bleed. Her hair was pulled back in a severe, glossy knot. She moved with the fluid grace of a simulation running on unlimited bandwidth.

"Detective Arrow," she said. Her voice was clear, perfectly modulated. "I was told you handle... semantic anomalies."

"I handle broken maps," Arrow said, gesturing to the chair. "Sit."

She sat. She didn’t fidget. She didn’t look around. Her eyes were a pale, unsettling violet.

"I am Elara Voss," she said. Or maybe she didn't. The name sounded right, but it slid off her tongue like it was borrowed. "I have a problem with my history."

Arrow tapped the Nexus interface. The blue eye flared brighter, beginning its passive scan. "We all have history problems, Ms. Voss. Usually, it’s about what we want to forget. I assume you’re the opposite?"

"I remember everything," she said. "I can recite the weather data for every day of the last ten years. I can recall every conversation, every transaction, every meal."

"But?"

"But I don't know how I *felt* about any of it."

Arrow stopped tapping. He looked at her. Really looked. The surface structure of her statement was clean. Subject, verb, negation. But the Meta Model violation was glaring.

*Deletion.*

"You have the data," Arrow said slowly. "But no metadata. No emotional tagging."

"It’s like reading a manual for a life I didn't live," she said. Her face remained perfectly smooth. No micro-tremor in the lip. No constriction of the pupils. "I need you to find the missing file. The Deep Structure."

Arrow leaned back. This was usually the part where he’d start the calibration. He’d throw a verbal jab, a provocation to spike the client's cortisol and get a baseline reading.

"Maybe you’re just bored," he said, his voice dropping to a gravelly rasp. "Maybe you’re a rich tourist from the Upper Spire looking for a tragedian narrative to spice up a dull Tuesday."

He watched her. He waited for the flash of irritation, the defensive crossing of arms, the slight flush of the neck.

Nothing.

"I am not bored," she said. The waveform of her voice was a flat line. "I am hollow."

Arrow glanced at the Nexus screen. The AI was churning through the linguistic patterns. The output scrolling on the monitor was a wall of green text: *SYNTAX: OPTIMAL. GRAMMAR: PERFECT. AMBIGUITY: 0%.*

It was too clean. It was unnatural.

*Nexus Nudge,* the screen flashed. A prompt appeared in the center of the chaos.

**NEXUS:** *You are auditing the accounts, but missing the bankruptcy. Scan for Zero-Point Deletions.*

Arrow frowned. Zero-Point Deletion. That wasn't a standard filter. It meant information hadn't just been left out; it had been erased so completely that the vacuum itself was the only evidence.

He looked back at Elara. He needed to break the surface. He needed to find the crack in the porcelain.

"Ms. Voss," Arrow said, leaning forward, invading her personal space with his gaze. "Tell me about the day you decided to come here. Not the logistics. The moment you knew you were broken."

"I woke up," she said. "The sun was hitting the wall. It was 0700 hours. I felt..." She paused. For the first time, a hesitation. "I determined that I required assistance."

"No," Arrow pressed. "Tell me about the day you lost it. The day the feelings stopped."

He pushed the question like a physical weight. He watched her throat. He watched the pulse point at her wrist.

Elara stared at him. "I... I do not have a record of a specific cessation date. It is a gradient descent."

"Try harder," Arrow commanded. "Close your eyes. Go back. Find a memory that *should* hurt. A death. A heartbreak. A failure."

She closed her eyes. The room went silent. The rain lashed against the window, harder now, a chaotic rhythm against the stillness of the woman in the chair.

Arrow watched the Nexus readout. He was looking for a spike. A jagged red line of adrenaline or grief.

Elara sat there for a long minute. Then she opened her eyes.

"I am thinking of my mother’s funeral," she said. "It was raining. Like this. The coffin was ebony."

"And?" Arrow whispered.

"And the wood grain was simulated oak," she said. "The floral arrangements were lilies. Cost: four hundred credits."

She looked at him, and her eyes were terrible in their emptiness. "There is nothing else, Detective. Just the data."

Arrow felt a chill that had nothing to do with the drafty window. He looked at the Nexus screen.

**NEXUS:** *DEEP STRUCTURE: NULL. SUBJECT IS VOID.*

She wasn't lying. She wasn't suppressing. She was empty.

"I'll take the case," Arrow said, his voice rougher than he intended.

Elara Voss stood up. She nodded, once. "Thank you. I will transfer the retainer."

She turned and walked out. The door chimed again as she left, a cheerful sound that felt obscene in the wake of her silence.

Arrow sat alone in the dim office. He looked at the chair where she had sat. It looked exactly the same. She had left no impression. No warmth.

He turned to the window. The rain was still falling. He stared at the glass, daring the face to come back. Daring the scream to return.

For a moment, the reflection of his own face in the dark glass seemed to flicker. The eyes... were they his eyes? Or were they just optical data arranged in a familiar pattern?

His hand reached for the pack of cigarettes on the desk. He pulled one out, and he saw it—the tremor. A fine, high-frequency shake in his fingers.

He lit the cigarette. The smoke curled up, grey and shapeless, drifting into the air conditioning vents.

"The map is not the territory," he whispered, the old Agency mantra.

But as he looked at the trembling smoke, Detective Arrow had the sinking realization that for the first time in years, he didn't know which one he was standing on.
